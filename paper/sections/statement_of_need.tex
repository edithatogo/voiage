\section{Information measures}

\subsection{Perfect, partial, and sample information}

Let $NB_{sd}$ denote the net benefit for probabilistic sample $s$ and strategy
$d$. For $S$ samples and $D$ strategies, the sample EVPI estimator is
\begin{equation}\label{eq:evpi}
  \widehat{\mathrm{EVPI}} =
  \frac{1}{S}\sum_{s=1}^{S}\max_{d} NB_{sd}
  - \max_{d}\frac{1}{S}\sum_{s=1}^{S} NB_{sd}.
\end{equation}
It estimates the expected reduction in decision loss if all uncertainty
represented by the simulation could be resolved. Under representative
independent and identically distributed draws with finite expectations, the
plug-in estimator is consistent. In finite samples it is generally
downward-biased because the maximum of estimated strategy means is
upward-biased. Monte Carlo uncertainty provides context for the estimate.

EVPPI restricts perfect information to a parameter subset $\phi$ and integrates
over the complementary uncertainty $\theta_{-\phi}$,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:evppi}
  \mathrm{EVPPI}(\phi) =
  \mathbb{E}_{\phi}\!\left[\max_d
  \mathbb{E}_{\theta_{-\phi}\mid\phi}
  \{NB_d(\phi,\theta_{-\phi})\}\right]
  - \max_d \mathbb{E}_{\theta}\{NB_d(\theta)\}.
\end{equation}
Regression from a probabilistic sensitivity analysis sample is one common
estimator~\cite{strong2014evppi}. EVSI instead values a specified finite study
with data $Y$,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:evsi}
  \mathrm{EVSI}(Y) =
  \mathbb{E}_{Y\sim p(Y)}\!\left[\max_d
  \mathbb{E}_{\theta\mid Y}\{NB_d(\theta)\}\right]
  - \max_d \mathbb{E}_{\theta}\{NB_d(\theta)\}.
\end{equation}
Its interpretation depends on the likelihood, prior, allocation, outcomes, and
decision rule used to describe the study. Regression reuse of existing
simulation output can reduce computational cost, while decision-theoretic
study design can compare sample sizes against their
costs~\cite{strong2015evsi,willan2005trial}.

\subsection{Population, implementation, and timing}

For per-decision, per-period EVSI $v$, recurring population $N$, horizon $T$,
annual discount rate $r$, and total research cost $C$, \texttt{voiage} defines
\begin{equation}\label{eq:enbs}
  \mathrm{ENBS}_{\mathrm{population}} =
  vN\left(\frac{1-{(1+r)}^{-T}}{r}\right)-C,
\end{equation}
with the annuity factor replaced by $T$ when $r=0$. This form assumes constant
recurring decision opportunities, end-of-period discounting, no population
change, and no implementation delay. Applying it to values already aggregated
over population or time would double-count that scaling. ENBS remains signed;
$\max(0,\mathrm{ENBS})$ is the separate value of the option to commission the
study.

Information value and realised value are not identical. Partial uptake,
adherence, coverage, or delayed results reduce the number of decisions affected
by new evidence. Implementation activity may therefore be more valuable than
another study when evidence is adequate but delivery is
weak~\cite{fenwick2008implementation}. Population scaling also depends on
eligibility, decision lifetime, displacement by later technologies, and the
delay before results become available. Applications with changing populations
can replace the simple annuity in~\eqref{eq:enbs} with an appropriate sequence
of decision opportunities.

Waiting can itself have value when a decision is reversible or commitment can
be delayed. Real-options analysis represents the interaction between
uncertainty, timing, and irreversibility rather than treating delay as a simple
discount adjustment~\cite{palmer2000options}. Sequential and adaptive analyses
similarly compare study designs and stopping rules through expected net
benefit~\cite{flight2022sequential}.

\subsection{Model, population, and perspective}

Parameter VOI is conditional on the model being an adequate account of the
decision. Structural VOI asks whether plausible model forms could change
action~\cite{jackson2011structural}. Transportability asks whether evidence
from a source population can inform the target decision under stated causal
assumptions~\cite{bareinboim2016datafusion}. Subgroup analysis examines whether
observable heterogeneity supports different decisions, while distributional
analysis changes the social objective through explicit equity
weights~\cite{espinoza2014heterogeneity,asaria2016distributional}. Perspective can
change which outcomes, costs, constraints, populations, and time horizons enter
the comparison~\cite{mordaunt2025vop}.

\subsection{Calculation and checks}

Software implementations can differ in sample orientation, missing-value
handling, tie rules, and approximations. \texttt{voiage} retains labels for
samples, strategies, uncertain inputs, subgroups, perspectives, populations,
and study designs using labelled data conventions~\cite{hoyer2017xarray}.
Detailed results can include the preferred strategy, method, random seed,
warnings, scaling assumptions, and data sources.

Checks cover invariance to a common shift in net benefit, non-negativity where
required by the estimand, repeatability, identical strategies, and malformed
inputs. Approximate estimates can still be affected by Monte Carlo error, poor
model fit, or extrapolation. Selected established numerical components are
implemented in Rust and exposed through the supported language packages.
Python currently supports the widest workflow; the R and Julia packages
provide narrower subsets.
