\section{Statement of need}

VOI analysis is central to deciding whether additional research is worthwhile,
but practical implementations are often fragmented across domain-specific
packages, languages, and model formats. This fragmentation complicates
comparison and can obscure which uncertainty is being valued. A parameter-level
EVPPI does not answer whether the model structure is wrong; an EVSI does not by
itself account for incomplete implementation; and population-average VOI can
hide subgroup-specific decisions or equity trade-offs. Structural uncertainty,
heterogeneity, and implementation are established methodological concerns, not
software edge cases~\cite{jackson2011structural,espinoza2014heterogeneity,fenwick2008implementation}.

\texttt{voiage} addresses five connected decision questions:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item \emph{parameter and sample information}: which uncertain inputs or
  proposed studies could change the preferred strategy;
  \item \emph{model and evidence adequacy}: whether structural assumptions,
  validation data, measurement quality, or transportability warrant further
  investment;
  \item \emph{population and social value}: whether subgroup heterogeneity,
  preferences, equity weights, or analytical perspective alter the decision;
  \item \emph{delivery}: whether uptake, adherence, coverage, delay, or
  implementation uncertainty erodes expected value; and
  \item \emph{timing and control}: whether sequential learning, adaptive
  sampling, waiting, irreversibility, or portfolio constraints change when to
  act.
\end{enumerate}

This taxonomy is domain-neutral, but its inputs retain domain meaning. Health
analyses can use net monetary or health benefit, willingness-to-pay thresholds,
eligible populations, and trial costs. Environmental analyses can compare
monitoring or mitigation strategies under ecological and monetary
consequences. Engineering and manufacturing analyses can value reliability,
failure, inspection, or quality information. Financial and energy analyses can
value delayed commitment, changing evidence, and irreversible investment. The
common requirement is an explicit mapping from uncertain states and strategies
to comparable decision value; the package does not pretend that the mapping is
scientifically interchangeable across domains.

Let $NB_{sd}$ denote the net benefit for probabilistic sample $s$ and strategy
$d$. For $S$ samples and $D$ strategies, the sample EVPI estimator is
\begin{equation}
  \widehat{\mathrm{EVPI}} =
  \frac{1}{S}\sum_{s=1}^{S}\max_{d} NB_{sd}
  - \max_{d}\frac{1}{S}\sum_{s=1}^{S} NB_{sd}.
\end{equation}
This is the expected opportunity loss of acting with current information.
EVPPI restricts perfect information to a parameter subset $\phi$ and requires
conditional expected net benefits,
\begin{equation}
  \mathrm{EVPPI}(\phi) =
  \mathbb{E}_{\phi}\!\left[\max_d
  \mathbb{E}(NB_d\mid\phi)\right]
  - \max_d \mathbb{E}(NB_d),
\end{equation}
which is commonly estimated using regression from a probabilistic sensitivity
analysis sample~\cite{strong2014evppi}. EVSI instead values a proposed finite
study $Y$ by averaging the decision made after observing its data,
\begin{equation}
  \mathrm{EVSI}(Y) =
  \mathbb{E}_{Y}\!\left[\max_d
  \mathbb{E}(NB_d\mid Y)\right]
  - \max_d \mathbb{E}(NB_d).
\end{equation}
Regression-based reuse of existing simulation output can reduce the cost of
this calculation~\cite{strong2015evsi}. For a population-level EVSI $V$ and
research cost $C$, \texttt{voiage} defines $\mathrm{ENBS}=V-C$.

These estimands are simple to state but easy to implement inconsistently.
Examples include transposing samples and strategies, accepting non-finite
values, changing tie-breaking rules, or silently switching approximation
methods; \texttt{voiage} therefore treats dimensions, finite-value checks,
method metadata, warnings, seeds, and serialized result envelopes as part of
the public numerical contract.

The same principle extends beyond classical estimands. For example,
distributional analysis compares a single population-wide decision with
subgroup-specific optima and an explicitly equity-weighted social-welfare
decision~\cite{asaria2016distributional}. Implementation-adjusted analysis
modifies expected net benefits for uptake, adherence, coverage, delay, and
implementation uncertainty rather than assuming instantaneous perfect
adoption. Sequential and adaptive analyses compare study designs and stopping
rules through their expected net benefit of sampling, consistent with the
decision-theoretic role of EVSI in trial design~\cite{flight2022sequential}.
