\section{Introduction}

Value of Information (VOI) analysis asks which uncertainty matters to a
decision, what evidence could reduce it, and whether the expected improvement
justifies the cost of
learning~\cite{claxton1999irrelevance,briggs2006decision}. In health technology
assessment this may mean
choosing a trial, targeting a subgroup, validating a prediction model, or
improving uptake. The formulation can also be used for environmental
monitoring, engineering reliability, manufacturing quality, energy planning,
and investment decisions when uncertain consequences are mapped to a common
decision-value scale.

The difficult part is usually not the textbook expected value of perfect
information (EVPI) equation. An analysis also involves choices about what
counts as a strategy, whose outcomes matter, which population and time horizon
to use, whether a model form is credible, whether evidence transports to the
target setting, and whether a recommendation is likely to be implemented.
Different choices can change
the preferred action even when the same simulation output is used. The
\texttt{voiage} package records these choices alongside the analysis and its
results instead of leaving them hidden in data preparation or analyst notes.

The paper follows four organising principles:
\begin{itemize}
  \item separating the value of resolving uncertainty from the value of
  improving implementation, so that more research is not automatically treated
  as the right response to a poor decision outcome;
  \item relating individual-level information value to the affected
  population, decision lifetime, discounting, research cost, and delay;
  \item making heterogeneity, equity, perspective, model structure, and
  transportability part of the decision question rather than hidden analytical
  choices; and
  \item distinguishing established calculations from developing extensions so
  that a computable result is not mistaken for a validated one.
\end{itemize}

The implemented methods include EVPI, expected value of partial perfect
information (EVPPI), expected value of sample information (EVSI), expected net
benefit of sampling (ENBS), dominance, the cost-effectiveness acceptability
frontier (CEAF), heterogeneity, and structural VOI\@. Newer features address
equity, perspective, implementation, validation, transportability, data
quality, and staged decisions. Version 1.0.0 is available for applied analysis
in Python, with narrower R and Julia packages. Definitions
and abbreviations are collected in Sections~\ref{sec:glossary}
and~\ref{sec:abbreviations}.
