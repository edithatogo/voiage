\section{Worked health example}

\subsection{Decision and study model}

Consider a health service deciding whether to fund a new programme instead of
standard care. The synthetic probabilistic model assigns the programme an
incremental quality-adjusted life-year (QALY) effect
$\theta\sim\mathcal{N}(0.060,0.030^2)$ and incremental cost
$C\sim\mathcal{N}(3000,650^2)$. The inputs are independent and the Normal
distributions are not truncated. These assumptions illustrate the analysis;
they are not estimates for an actual intervention. The decision model uses
$10\,000$ draws and values one QALY at $50\,000$ value units.

The proposed study observes the difference in mean QALYs between two
equal-sized arms. Conditional on the true incremental effect,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:health-likelihood}
  \bar{Y}\mid\theta,n \sim
  \mathcal{N}\!\left(\theta,\frac{4\sigma_Y^2}{n}\right),
\end{equation}
where $n$ is total sample size and the common individual outcome standard
deviation $\sigma_Y$ is 1.0 QALY\@. Conjugate Normal updating gives a
preposterior variance for the posterior mean of
\begin{equation}\label{eq:health-preposterior}
  \operatorname{Var}_{\bar{Y}}
  \!\left\{\mathbb{E}(\theta\mid\bar{Y})\right\}
  =
  \frac{\tau^4}{\tau^2+4\sigma_Y^2/n},
\end{equation}
where $\tau=0.030$ is the prior standard deviation. The study informs the
health effect but not cost, which remains integrated at its prior mean when
the post-study decision is made. This analytic conjugate Normal calculation
avoids Monte Carlo error in EVSI for the declared study model.

\subsection{Results}

At $50\,000$ per QALY, the new programme is preferred in 49.24\% of the
probabilistic draws (Monte Carlo standard error 0.50 percentage points; 95\%
percentile bootstrap interval 48.19\% to 50.21\%). EVPI is 644 value units per
person (95\% percentile bootstrap interval 624 to 658). EVPPI is 590 for the health effect
(569 to 603) and 250 for programme cost (229 to 265), indicating that
health-effect uncertainty accounts for more of the avoidable loss in this
model. The intervals describe simulation uncertainty conditional on the
declared model and do not represent uncertainty about the model assumptions.
Partial values are not additive, so their sum is not interpreted as EVPI\@.

Per-person EVSI rises from 63 at a total sample size of 50 to 276 at $1\,200$. With
immediate results and full uptake, population ENBS changes from negative to
positive between 100 and 200 participants. With a two-year delay and 60\%
uptake, it remains negative at 800 and becomes positive by $1\,200$. These
crossovers follow from the assumptions in Table~\ref{tab:health-assumptions};
they are not general sample-size recommendations.

A prespecified one-way sensitivity analysis varies the outcome standard
deviation from 1.0 to 0.75 and 1.25 QALYs, annual population from 1,300 to
1,000 and 1,600, and fixed study cost from 1.2 million to 0.9 and 1.5 million
value units. Across these scenarios, the first evaluated sample size with
positive ENBS ranges from 100 to 400. Separate implementation scenarios move
that point to 400 participants with a one-year delay and 80\% uptake, and
beyond 1,200 participants with a three-year delay and 40\% uptake. These
comparisons show that the study-value conclusion is sensitive to assumptions
governing both information gain and the number of decisions that can benefit.

\subsection{Interpretation}

Panel A shows that the decision is most uncertain around the reference
threshold. Panel B identifies the health effect as the more valuable evidence
target under the stated model. Panel C shows that the same study can have
different population value when fewer future decisions benefit from its
results. Study information, research cost, timing, and uptake therefore enter
the investment comparison separately.
