\section{Decision-problem architecture}

\subsection{Established and developing methods}

\texttt{voiage} does not present every available method as equally established.
Stable methods have documented behaviour that is intended to remain compatible
across releases. Developing methods have documented inputs, examples, and
repeatable test results, but still need wider methodological and applied
evidence. Experimental features can change.

\subsection{Keeping the meaning of the data visible}

A \texttt{ValueArray} records values with named sample and strategy dimensions
and can also retain labels for different viewpoints. It can be accompanied by
the uncertain inputs, study design, available choices, and relevant population.
This reduces the risk that rows or columns silently change meaning when the
same data move between an EVPI calculation, subgroup analysis, or comparison
of viewpoints. This approach follows the labelled-data convention represented
by \texttt{xarray}~\cite{hoyer2017xarray}.

Classical VOI functions can return a single numerical estimate. Where
supported, fuller results also record the preferred choice, comparison,
calculation method, development status, warnings, and assumptions needed to
explain that estimate. Population size, time horizon, discounting,
implementation assumptions, subgroup weights, data-quality adjustments, and
decision timing remain visible where relevant. The software reports invalid
shapes, missing values, inconsistent labels, and impossible rates rather than
quietly continuing.

\subsection{Worked numerical example}

For two strategies with net-benefit rows $(0,100)$ and $(0,-100)$, the sample
EVPI is 50 value units. For a proposed study with per-decision EVSI 12.5 and
total research cost 5, signed ENBS is 7.5. If the EVSI recurs for 1000 annual
decisions over 10 years at a 3\% annual discount rate, the annuity factor is
8.5302028368 and population ENBS is
$12.5\times1000\times8.5302028368-5=106622.53546$ value units. These
calculations distinguish EVPI from EVSI, preserve negative ENBS when research
is not worthwhile, and subtract total research cost only after population
scaling. Automated tests check the same calculation in both Rust and Python.

\subsection{Software design and analytical checks}

Rust performs selected established calculations. Python supports user-supplied
models and the broader analysis workflow, including data checks, labelled data,
plots, and reports. Keeping selected calculations in one shared component helps
Python, R, and Julia produce the same result. The current R and Julia packages
calculate EVPI directly but do not yet provide the wider Python feature set.

Analytical assurance targets decision properties rather than only code
execution. Tests cover translation and positive-scaling invariance,
non-negativity where required, repeatable use of random numbers, difficult
statistical models, identical choices, malformed inputs, and agreement between
languages. Exact calculations are checked against their mathematical
properties. Approximate EVPPI calculations can fall outside theoretical bounds
because of simulation error, a poor-fitting model, or predictions beyond the
observed data; such results require investigation.
Property-based, metamorphic, fuzz, mutation, and integration tests complement
example-based checks. These test specified behavior of the implemented
estimators; they cannot validate a user's model structure, evidence, or value
judgments.
